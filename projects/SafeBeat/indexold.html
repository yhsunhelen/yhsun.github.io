Project

SafeBeat

A research-driven ECG monitoring wearable concept for older adults living alone, combining real-time sensing with approachable, low-burden feedback.

Design boards · Prototype flow · Scenario storyboard

Abstract

SafeBeat explores how an ECG monitoring device can better fit the routines and constraints of older adults who live independently and may experience chronic cardiovascular risk. The project emphasizes low-friction use, clear interpretation, and wearability over extended periods.

Design contribution

Translating clinical-style monitoring into an everyday object by prioritizing portability, intuitive status signals, and a feedback loop that reduces user burden while supporting timely attention to abnormal patterns.

Context & Motivation

Problem framing and market landscape.

The design direction is driven by the gap between “professional monitoring” and “everyday understanding.” While existing technologies can measure heart activity, the experience often breaks down at comprehension, day-to-day comfort, and sustained adoption—especially for users who may not self-identify as “tech users.”

User Research

Interview synthesis, persona, and design insights.

Key needs distilled

  • Low-burden monitoring: automatic, continuous data capture with minimal steps.
  • Clarity over raw data: interpretation that feels actionable, not intimidating.
  • Wear comfort: materials and form that remain comfortable during prolonged wear.
  • Trust and reassurance: a device that reduces anxiety rather than amplifying it.

Design principles

  • Portability: unobtrusive and easy to carry/wear in daily routines.
  • Readable signals: simplified status cues (e.g., color states) to lower cognitive load.
  • Gentle interaction: surfaces and edges that communicate safety and calm.

Concept Development

Form exploration and interaction clarity.

Interaction language

The concept favors an immediately understandable feedback system: green indicates normal operation, orange requests attention, and red suggests rest or seeking guidance. This supports quick comprehension without requiring the user to interpret clinical charts.

Wearability decisions

  • Rounded, low-aggression form to enhance perceived safety.
  • Directional affordances (surface details) to reduce “which way is up?” confusion.
  • Replaceable contact parts to support hygiene and maintenance over time.

Prototype & System

Signal pipeline and demo validation.

System pipeline (conceptual)

  1. ECG signal acquisition via wearable contact/patch.
  2. Real-time transmission to an analysis module.
  3. Pattern analysis and status classification.
  4. Simple alerts and guidance to reduce uncertainty.

Feedback loop highlights

  • Issue: misjudgment of “normal” under automatic detection in some conditions.
  • Response: improve interpretation by incorporating contextual data features.
  • Issue: the patch may loosen during wear.
  • Response: explore alternative fixation methods (e.g., armband) for stability.
